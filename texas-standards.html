What Texas law actually says

The short version: operate as a private school, teach five subjects, and keep your own records. That's it.

Texas Education Code § 25.086(a)(1)

The compulsory attendance exemption

A child is exempt from compulsory attendance if the child "attends a private or parochial school that includes in its course study reading, spelling, grammar, mathematics, and good citizenship." Texas courts have confirmed that a home is a private school. No registration, no filing, no state permission is required.

1

Operate as a bona fide school

Your home school must be conducted in good faith. This means following an actual curriculum, maintaining some structure, and keeping basic records — not necessarily a rigid schedule, but genuine instruction.

OatSmart's lesson planner, assignment tracker, and calendar create a natural paper trail that demonstrates bona fide operation.
2

Teach the five required subjects

Texas requires instruction in: Reading, Spelling, Grammar, Mathematics, and Good Citizenship. The law does not specify how much time, what curriculum to use, or how to assess mastery.

You can tag all five subjects in OatSmart and log assignments for each. Reports show coverage at a glance.
3

No state registration required

You do not register with the TEA, the local school district, or any state office. You do not need to notify anyone that you are homeschooling. Simply withdraw from public school (if applicable) and begin.

4

No testing or portfolio submission required

Texas does not require standardized testing, end-of-year assessments, or portfolio reviews. You keep records for your own use — not for submission to any authority.

TEKS — Texas curriculum standards

The TEKS (Texas Essential Knowledge and Skills) are the state's curriculum guidelines for public schools. Homeschoolers are not required to follow them — but they're a useful reference for knowing what grade-level learning looks like in Texas.

Record-keeping in Texas

Texas law does not specify what records you must keep or for how long. But good records protect you, help your children, and make college applications or re-enrollment into public school much easier.

1

Attendance & school days

Log the days you school. Texas does not specify a minimum number of days, but having a record demonstrates you operated as a bona fide school. Most families aim for 170–180 days, mirroring the public school calendar.

Reading portfolio

A reading log builds a meaningful portfolio over time. List titles, dates, and reading levels. For older students, include brief narrations or book reports.

Report cards & transcripts

Not required by Texas law, but invaluable for high schoolers applying to college, dual enrollment programs, or the military. A well-formatted transcript carries the same weight as a public school record.

Common questions

Things Texas homeschool families ask us regularly — answered plainly.

Does the school district have authority over my homeschool?
Generally, no. The Leeper v. Arlington ISD (1994) case established that homeschools in Texas are private schools and are exempt from state compulsory attendance laws when they meet the five-subject requirement. School districts do not have the authority to inspect, test, or require reports from homeschool families.
What if the district sends me a truancy notice?
If you receive a truancy notice, respond in writing explaining that your child is enrolled in a private home school that meets the requirements of Texas Education Code § 25.086. You are not required to provide curriculum plans, test scores, or portfolio materials. Organizations like THSC (Texas Home School Coalition) can provide legal support if needed.
Can my child re-enroll in public school after homeschooling?
Yes. Texas public schools must accept homeschooled students. The school may give placement tests to determine grade level and may not give full credit for homeschool coursework, though many do when presented with a transcript. A well-maintained OatSmart transcript significantly smooths this process.
Can my homeschooled child attend community college in Texas?
Yes. Texas community colleges accept homeschool graduates. Requirements vary by institution but typically include a homeschool transcript, a diploma (which you as the parent can issue), and placement testing. Some schools also accept SAT/ACT scores in lieu of placement tests.
Do I need to issue a diploma?
Texas does not require a diploma to homeschool, but if your child plans to attend college, join the military, or enter certain licensed professions, a diploma helps. As the administrator of your private school, you have the authority to issue one. OatSmart's transcript builder produces documentation that supports your diploma.
What about students with learning differences or IEPs?
Homeschooled children with disabilities are not entitled to special education services from their local school district in Texas, as they are enrolled in a private school. However, some districts offer limited services voluntarily. Families who want IDEA protections may consider partial enrollment. This is an area where consulting a special education attorney is valuable.
